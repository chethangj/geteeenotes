CHAPTER 03 : DISTRIBUTION SYSTEMS

Distribution System:

It is a part of electrical power system network which distributes electrical power to the consumers for general utilization.

From generating station to the receiving station power is brought by using transmission. From secondary substation power is brought upto the consumers meter board by an electric conductor called distribution system.

Classification of distribution system:

  1. According to the nature of current
    1. DC distribution system
      1. 2 wire DC system
      2. 3 wire DC system
    2. AC distribution system
      1. Primary distribution system
      2. Secondary distribution system
  2. According to the scheme of connection
    1. Radial distribution system
    2. Ring main distribution system
    3. Interconnected distribution system
  3. According to the constructional arrangements
    1. Overhead distribution system
    2. Underground distribution system

Single line diagram of distribution system:

The distribution line can be subdivided into three parts namely,

  1. Feeders
  2. Second distributors
  3. Service mains

Feeders:

Feeders are lines or conductors which connects substations and distribution substation or localized generating station to the area where electrical power is to be distributed.

Generally loading current of a feeder uniform along the length of the line since no tapings are taken from the feeder. The feeder carries current in large capacity and design of a feeder mainly depends on the current to be carried. The feeder lines can be overhead and underground.

Distributor:

Distributors are the line or conductors which run along streets to be supply electrical power to the consumers.

The numbers of tapings are taken from the distributors for supplying electrical power to the consumer. Hence current in the distributors is non-uniform. It varies along its length of the line. While designing the distributor lines voltage drop is considered as a main factor.

Service mains:

The service mains are the connecting link between nearby distributor and consumer terminals. For service main insulated cable is used and it may be UG or OH.

Radial Distribution System:

In radial system of distribution no. of separate feeders are radiated from common single substation/ hence it is called radial system.

There is combination of one distributor fed by one feeder connecting that feeder to the substation.

In the above figure distributor one is connected only at one end of substation through a feeder at point a similarly the other feeder is feeding the distributor two only at one point B.

Due to such system, if the fault occurs either on feeder or a distributor, all the consumers connected to that distributor will get affected. They would be an interruption of supply to all such consumers.

Similarly the end of the distributor near to the substation will get heavily loaded than the end which is too far away from the substation.

Similarly the consumers at the end of the distributor would be subjected to the voltage fluctuations has the load on distributor changes.

This type of distribution system is used for low voltage, medium density load areas and where substation is located at the center of the load.

Advantages of Radial Distribution System:

  • Simple in construction and circuit arrangements.
  • Useful when the generator is at low voltage.
  • Initial cost is low.

Disadvantages of Radial Distribution System:

  • This system suitable for short distance distribution only.
  • When load variation on the distribution takes places, there is a serious voltage fluctuation for consumers at the far end of the distribution.
  • The consumers attached to one feeder and one distributor depends only on the reliability of that feeder and distributor. If any fault takes place in that feeder or distributor it cuts off supply to the all consumers.

Ring Main Distribution System:

  • This type of distribution system eliminates the disadvantages of the radial system.
  • The feeder covers the whole area of supply in the ring passion and finally terminates at the substation from where it is started. The feeder is in closed loop form and looks like a ring hence it is called as ring main system.
  • The feeder in the ring is divided into no. of sections as AB, BC, CD, DE and EA. The various distributors are connected at point A, B, C, D and E.
  • Each distributor is supplied by the two feeders. If there is any fault on any part of the feeder still the consumers will keep on getting the continuous supply from another feeder. Then the faulted feeder can be isolated and repaired.

Advantages of Ring Main Distribution System:

  • Less fluctuation at consumer side.
  • Compare to radial system the voltage drop is less.
  • Efficiency of the system is high.
  • Increases the reliability of the supply as feeder is fed from two sides.
  • Peak load can be shared.

Disadvantages of Ring Main Distribution System:

  • Initial cost is higher.
  • Design is complicated.
  • Higher maintenance coast.

Inter Connected System:

The inter connection of different generating stations in parallel so to transfer power from one part to other during normal and emergency conditions is called interconnected system. This is also called as national grid.

In such interconnected system the feeder is energized by two or more stations. This ensures reliability and security of an electric supply.

The figure shows an interconnected distribution system. The feeder ring ABC is supply from two substations S1 and S2 at the point B and C. the distributors are connected to the point P, Q and R.

If there is any problem to one substation, supply can be maintained from other substation.

Advantages of Inter Connected System:

  • Increases reliability of the supply.
  • Peak load can be adjusted.
  • Increases the efficiency of the system,
  • Reduces the cost of reserve capacity.
  • Stations are operated economically.
  • Low capital cost.

Disadvantages of Inter Connected System

  • The overall system becomes complicated.
  • Stability problems due to inter connection.
  • Problem in maintaining system frequency.

AC Distribution:

The AC distribution is classified into two types.

  1. Primary Distribution
  2. Secondary Distribution

Primary Distribution:

At the substation the voltage level is reduced to 6.6 KV, 3.3 KV or 11 KV with the help of step down transformers. It uses three phase three wire underground system and the power is further transmitted to the local distribution centers. This primary distribution is also called as high voltage distribution. For the large consumers like factories and industries the power is directly transmitted to such loads from a substation. Such big loads have their own substations.

Secondary Distribution:

Secondary distribution is the part of AC power system network. Secondary distributors are those lines which distributes electrical energy in locations where direct consumers can use this energy. The secondary distribution is carried out by three phase four wire system. The secondary distribution lines are pulled from distribution substations of 11 KV to 230 V or 440 V and are run along the side of each and every road of the locality.

The voltage rating of secondary distribution system is 440 V between lines and 230 V between line and neutral.

The three phase loads are connected across the three phases either in star or delta.

DC Distribution System:

Dc distribution system is classified into two types.

  1. Two wire DC distribution system
  2. Three wire DC distribution system

Two wire DC distribution system:

This distribution system consists of two wires which are supplied by DC source. The outgoing wire is a positive wire and the return wire is a negative wire. The loads like lamps, motors etc. are connected in parallel between positive and negative wires. It is generally used for one standard value of low voltage.

Three wire DC distribution system:

This system consists of three wires, in which two wires are outers and one is neutral. The neutral wire is earthed at a substation. These three wires are supplied from DC source and two voltages can be obtained by this method.

The voltage 1 V is available between any one outer wire and a neutral then voltage available between two outer will be 2 V that us double of the voltage between outer and neutral. The low voltage loads like lamps, domestic appliances are connected between outer and a neutral. And high voltage loads like motors etc. are connected between two outers.

DC distribution calculation:

While designing distribution line the main factor considered is a voltage drop. The voltage drop depends upon a nature of the current loading on the distributor and the way of feeding.

  1. According to the loading the distributor are classified as,
    1. Concentrated loading
    2. Uniformly distributed loading
  2. According to the feeding the distributors are classified as,
    1. Fed at one end only
    2. Fed at both the ends
      1. At equal voltage
      2. At unequal voltage

Voltage drop calculation:

a) Two wire DC distributor fed at one end with concentrated load:

The above figure shows a single line diagram of a two wire DC distributor fed at one end F.

Let the concentrated loads I1, I2, I3 and I4 at point A, B, C and D.

Therefore,

Current in CD, ICD=I4
Current in BC, IBC=I3+ I4
Current in AB, IAB= I2+ I3+ I4
Current in FA, IFA=I1+ I2+ I3+ I4

Let R1, R2, R3 and R4 be the resistance of two wires that is outgoing and return for a section FA, AB, BC and CD of the distributor.

Therefore, voltage drop in section FA,

\[{V_{FA}} = {I_{FA}} \times {R_{FA}}\]
\[{V_{FA}} = \left( {{I_1} + {I_2} + {I_3} + {I_4}} \right) \times {R_1}\]

Similarly in section AB,

\[{V_{AB}} = \left( {{I_2} + {I_3} + {I_4}} \right) \times {R_2}\]
\[{V_{BC}} = \left( {{I_3} + {I_4}} \right) \times {R_3}\]
\[{V_{CD}} = \left( {{I_4}} \right) \times {R_4}\]

Therefore voltage drop in complete distributor FD is,

\[{V_{FD}} = \left( {{I_1} + {I_2} + {I_3} + {I_4}} \right) \times {R_1}) + \left( {\left( {{I_2} + {I_3} + {I_4}} \right) \times {R_2}} \right) + \left( {\left( {{I_3} + {I_4}} \right) \times {R_3}} \right) + \left( {\left( {{I_4}} \right) \times {R_4}} \right)\]

It is absorbed that minimum voltage will occur at point D which is farthest from feeding point F.

Distribution Automation:

It can be defined as automatically monitoring, protecting, regulating and controlling the various operations in a distribution system with the help of intelligent electronic devices under normal and abnormal conditions.

Objectives of distribution automation:

  • To improve the reliability of the distribution system.
  • Improve the quality of the service to reduce the peak loads to overcome power shortage.
  • Preventing theft of power.
  • To improve the financial performance of the overall system.
  • To minimize the power losses to network configuration to overcome the power shortage.
  • To meet the future demand with sufficient rescue margin.
  • To make this system self-reacting, self-configurable and self-organizing under various situations.

Functions of distribution automation:

The functions of distribution automation are divided as per various as,

1. Load Control:

  • Scheduling of load.
  • Reducing peak loads.
  • Preventing over loading of distribution.

2. Substation:

  • Monitoring of the status of substation equipments.
  • Monitoring voltage and load condition.
  • Supervisory control.
  • Collection of history of failures.

3. Feeder:

  • Identification of faults.
  • Monitoring current and voltage.
  • Collecting of information.
  • Isolation of faults and service restoration.
  • Reactive hold ampere control.

4. Controlling reactors and capacitors:

  • Automatic meter reading.
  • Monitoring proper operation of meter.
  • Disconnecting and reconnecting of remote service.

Benefits of distribution automation:

  • Identifying the fault in time and avoiding damage of equipments.
  • Improving the continuity of supply.
  • To save the labour cost.
  • To reduce the theft of power.
  • Efficient network maintain.
  • To prevent outages and reduces losses.
  • Increase transmission capacity.
  • To improve the PF and voltage profile.

Problems with existing distribution system:

  • Every year a load demand is increasing very rapidly hence it becomes necessary to install additional facility to meet increasing demand. This is very difficult to archive in practice.
  • The present losses in distribution system are very high and it is necessary to reduce those losses.
  • The rate of equipment failure is high due to overload conditions.
  • Load controls of agricultural pumps are required as those pumps contribute for 80% of the peak demand.
  • The present PF of the system is low hence efficient reactive power is necessary.
  • Satisfaction voltage profile system

Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition System (SCADA):

  • SCADAS stands for supervisory control and data acquisition system.
  • It can be defines as a computer system used for monitoring and controlling the power plant and industrial processes.
  • The name itself indicates that SCADA is not a full control system. It controls the system at the supervisory level.
  • SCADA id purely a software package. This is used in industry power generating station, power transmission and engineering application to analyze, monitor and controls the distributed system from a central master location.

Needs for SCADA:

  • High level of complexity cannot be handled by humans.
  • Control of many processes simultaneously from single control room is necessary.
  • To improve the quality, perfect time line decisions are required.
  • To improve the reliability of service.
  • More complex systems are needed now a day to become more and more efficient.
  • To reduce the reserve requirement.

All these tasks can be managed by a computer system hence SCADA is necessary.

Functions of SCADA:

Functions of SCADA are,

  • The data collections which provides measurement and status information to the supervisory control.
  • Supervisory control enables operator to remotely control the devices like relays, circuit breakers, capacitor switches etc.
  • To permit the operators to indicate the operations at remote stations from a central control center.
  • Alarm sense in case of non-recommended functioning of any component in the system.
  • Monitoring of all the important points of the system such as substation loads, ON and OFF of transformers fans, switchgear etc.
  • Detecting the failure and errors in various communication channels.
  • Data acquisition from metering equipments.
  • Controlling and indicating the position of two or three position devices like circuit breaker.
  • Displaying the important information in various forms.
  • Initiation and recognition of sequence of events after data calculation.
  • Along with distribution automation, the SCADA system improves the reliability and efficiency of power distributor to the consumers reducing the operating cost.
  • Used for data logging.

Block diagram of SCADA:

The above figure shows the block diagram of simple SCADA system. It consists of following components.

Computer or human machine interface:

This is most important component. It presents the process information to a human operator and allows human operator to monitor and control the process. The man machine interface that is interaction between man and machine. It consists of scanners, data loggers, display, statistical processing, control regulation and optimization.

Supervisory system:

It acquires the data on the process, displays is as required and sends controlling commands to the process.

Communication interface:

The system which connects computer, supervisory system and on field devices like remote terminal unit is called communication interface.

Remote terminal unit (RTU):

The RTU’s connects the sensors to the process. They convert the sensing signals to digital data and send it to the computer system. A remote terminal unit is a microprocessor controlled electronic device that interfaces objects in the physical world to a distributed control system.

Programmable logic controller (PLC):

These are used as field devices. These are special unit which are more economical, flexible and configurable than RTU’s.

Auxiliary memory:

It is an additional memory unit used to store historical data and backup of day to day operations.

Advantages of SCADA:

  • It ensures reliability and quality of power supply.
  • Quick alternative arrangements for power can be made under emergency requirement.
  • Very fast restoration of system under abnormal conditions.
  • There is huge scope for future expansion.
  • Complete centralized control is possible.
  • Thought initial cost is high but it reduces the operating cost considerable.
  • The information storage, data calculation and decision making is highly reliable and accurate.
  • It reduces human interference and errors.

Disadvantages of SCADA:

  • Over all implementations cost is very high.
  • It increases the requirement skilled operator.
  • It increases the complexity of software as well as hardware.
