CHAPTER 04 : RECTIFIERS, FILTERS AND REGULATORS

Regulated Power Supply:

In the block diagram of regulated power supply input is connected to transformer which is step down AC voltage level to desired AC output voltage (230V). A diode rectifier then provides pulsating DC voltage which is filtered by a filter to produce DC output voltage.

The resulting output of a filter has some ripple (AC components). It is applied as input to the regulator.

Regulator circuit produces constant output even when the input voltage and load is varying.

Rectifier:

Rectifier is a device which converts AC voltage to pulsating DC using one or more PN junction.

Rectifiers are classified into two types.

  1. Half wave rectifier
  2. Full wave rectifier
    1. Full wave bridge rectifier
    2. Full wave center tapped rectifier

Half Wave Rectifier:

Fig (a): Circuit Diagram

Fig (b): Waveform

Semiconductor diode is connected in series with the load resistance and secondary winding of transformer.

Operation:

  • The AC cycle is applied to the primary winding of the transformer. During the +ve half cycle of the AC input end P becomes +ve and Q becomes -ve. This makes a diode forward bias when diode is connecting in forward bias it conducts. Hence we get positive cycle of the AC rectifier.
  • During –ve half cycle of AC, P becomes –ve and Q becomes +ve and diode is reverse biased.
  • During reverse bias of a diode it does not conduct hence negative half cycle of the AC is not rectified.
  • Therefore, we conclude that current flows through the diode during +ve half cycle of the AC only. It is blocked during the -ve half cycle hence a pulsating DC is obtain across RL.

Full Wave Center Tapped Rectifier:

Fig (a): Circuit Diagram

Fig (b): Waveform

It essentially consists of two diodes D1, D2 and load resistance RL is connected to the secondary center tap transformer. Input signal is applied to the primary winding of the transformer. The center tap of the secondary winding usually taken as a ground or 0 voltage.

Operation:

  • During the +ve half cycle of secondary voltage, P of the secondary winding becomes +ve and Q becomes –ve. This makes diode D1 forward biased and diode D2 reverse biased. Therefore D1 conducts while D2 does not conducts the conventional current flow.
  • During –ve half cycle the polarity of the secondary voltage changes that means during –ve half cycle, P becomes –ve and Q becomes +ve under this condition D2 conducts while D1 does not conduct.
  • From the above discussion we conclude the current flows through the load RL remains same for +ve and –ve half cycle of AC input voltage.
  • Therefore the pulsating DC voltage is obtained across load RL.

Forward Current:

It is a current flowing through a forward bias diode every diode has been design to withstand maximum value of forward current.

Peak Inverse Voltage:

It is a maximum inverse voltage that diode can withstand without destroying the junction.

Peak Inverse Voltage of Half Wave Rectifier:

During the –ve half cycle of the AC voltage diode is in reverse bias and it does not conduct. Thus the maximum value of reverse voltage occurs at the peak of the –ve input cycle which is equal to VM.

Peak Inverse Voltage of Full Wave Rectifier:

In such type of circuit one diode conducts while another diode doesn’t conduct. Therefore whole of the secondary voltage appear across the non-conducting diode. Ultimately peak inverse voltage is,

\[{V_M} + {V_M} = 2{V_M}\]

Full Wave Bridge Rectifier:

Fig (a): Circuit Diagram

Fig (b): +ve Half Cycle

Fig (c): -ve Half Cycle

Fig (a): Waveform

  • The need of s center taped transformer is eliminated in the bridge rectifier. It contains four diodes D1, D2, D3 and D4 connected to form a bridge.
  • The AC supply to be rectified is applied to diagonally opposite ends of the bridge through the transformer.
  • Between other two ends of the bridge the load RL is connected.

Operation:

  • During +ve half cycle of the AC input, point A becomes +ve and B becomes –ve. Diode D1 and D3 are forward biased and they conduct while D4 and D2 are reverse biased and they does not conduct.
  • Therefore these two diodes will be in series with resistance RL as shown in figure (a).
  • During –ve half cycle of the AC input, point A becomes –ve and B becomes +ve and diode D2 and D4 are in forward biased and they conducts and D1 and D3 are in reverse biased and they does not conduct.
  • Therefore D2 and D4 are in series with the resistance RL as shown in figure (b).

Average Values:

The average values of output voltage and load current are same as for center tap transformer rectifier.

Ripple Factor:

The output of the half wave rectifier is not a pure DC but a pulsating DC. The output containing pulsating components called ripple.

It is defined as the ratio of RMS value of AC component output to the average value of DC component of the output.

Ripple factor for half wave rectifier is 1.21.

Ripple factor for full wave rectifier is 0.48.

Filters:

Filter is a circuit which removes the unwanted AC components from rectifier output but allows only DC components to reach the load.

Necessity of Filers:

A filter consists of positive circuit elements such as resistors, inductors and capacitors.

The rectified output consists of unwanted AC components called ripple. These ripples are not useful for electronic circuits. To achieve pure DC a filter circuit is required.

Inductor Capacitor or Inverted L Type Filter:

  • In this type of filter the inductor and capacitor are connected in the form of inverted L shape hence the name is Inverted L shape filter.
  • The output of the rectifier is given as the input to the filter circuit.
  • The output of the rectifier contains some AC and DC components.
  • The choke offers high opposition to the AC component but allows only DC components.
  • At point M DC with less pulsation passes and AC component which are present at M passes through capacitor hence only DC reaches the load.

Pie Filter:

  • Figure shows the pie filter, capacitor C1 allows only AC components to pass through it hence DC passes through choke.
  • At point M if any AC component is left will be manage to pass through capacitor C2 hence only DC reaches the load.

Voltage Regulator:

Voltage regulator circuit is one which is design to keep the output voltage of a power supply nearly constant under varying input voltage and varying load conditions.

Operating voltages,

7809 = +9 V
7812 = +12V
7905 = -5V
7912 = -12V

Working of the 7805:

  • IC 7805 is connected to provide voltage regulation with output DC +5V. an un regulated input voltage Vi is filtered by input capacitor Cin Connected to ICin terminal pin 1. It is used to cancel the effect due to long distribution.
  • Another bypass capacitor Cout connected at ICout terminal pin 2 used as regulator. Output produces +5V to improve transient response.
  • The third IC terminal is connected to the ground.
