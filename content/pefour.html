CHAPTER 04 : CONVERTERS

Types of power electronic converters:

Power electronic converter consists of three types.

1. AC to DC converters (Rectifiers):

  • Input is single phase or three phase AC supply normally available from mains. Output is controlled DC voltage and DC current. Hence AC to DC converters includes diode rectifiers as well as controlled rectifiers. Controlled rectifiers uses the SCR, the firing angle of SCR is controlled to obtain variable DC output.
  • These converters are used in UPS, DC drivers and HVDC systems.

2. DC to AC converters (Inverters):

  • DC to AC converters is normally called inverters. The input is fixed DC voltage which is obtained from battery and output is variable voltage, variable frequency AC voltage. The firing angle of an SCR in inverter is controlled to obtain an rms output voltage.
  • These converters are used in AC drivers and speed control of induction motor.

3. DC to DC converters (Choppers):

  • DC to DC converters is also called as chopper. Input is fixed DC voltage obtained from battery and output is fixed or variable DC voltage.
  • The average value of output is varied by controlling ON time of chopper switches.
  • DC choppers are used in DC drivers, SMPS, battery chargers etc.

4. AC to AC converters (Cyclo converters);

  • The input is fixed AC voltage and fixed frequency and output is variable AC voltage and variable frequency.
  • Note that in cyclo converters output frequency is higher or lower than the input frequency.
  • Cyclo converters are used in AC traction drives.

5. AC to AC converters (AC regulators):

  • Input is fixed AC voltage, fixed frequency obtained from AC mains and output is variable AC voltage, fixed frequency which is suitable for load.
  • Note that in AC regulators output frequency is same as the input frequency. Therefore AC regulator does not change the frequency but cyclo converter can change the frequency.
  • AC regulators are used in speed control of large fans and pumps.

Advantages and disadvantages of power electronic converters:

Advantages:

  • Fast dynamic response due to static devices.
  • Compact in size and less weight of controllers due to use of electronic devices.
  • It increases the operating life and reduces the maintenance cost.
  • Operation highly flexible.
  • EMI (electromagnetic interference), acoustic noises are reduced.
  • Easy fault finding.
  • Quick assembly.

Disadvantages:

  • For very simple conversion power electronic converters may be costly. Therefore it is costly for low power applications.
  • It is not repairable.
  • It is not much suitable for high power applications.
  • Power electronic controllers generated the harmonics.

Smart or intelligent power modules:

  • The power electronic converters may require two, four or six devices sometimes additional feedback or freewheeling diodes are also required rather using this discrete components and devices. A combined module of all the devices is used. It is called power module.
  • Complete power converter is available in a single power module. This result in the power converters is very compact.
  • A smart power module includes power devices as well as peripheral circuit fabricated on single chip.
  • Smart power module also includes the interfacing of power devices with triggering and control circuit.
  • Smart power module also includes the drive circuit protection and diagnostic circuit, microcomputer control and power supply etc.
  • Smart power module is also includes the EMC unit to avoid the electromagnetic radiation.

Note definitions:

Firing angle (α):

Firing angle is the angle or delay in radians or degree when SCR is triggered after positive half cycle of AC supply voltage.

Therefore α varies from 0 to π or 0 to 1800.

Conduction angle (β):

Conduction angle is the period in radians or degree over which the SCR conducts.

Controlled rectifiers:

  • Here power transferred from source to load is controlled by controlling angle of the devices.
  • The firing angle or triggering angle of the device is controlled by control circuit.
  • Input is AC output is regulated or adjustable DC hence power transfer across the load is regulated.

Types of controlled rectifiers:

  1. Single Phase Rectifiers
    1. Single Phase Semi Converter
    2. Single Phase Full Converter
    3. Single Phase Dual Converter
  2. Three Phase Rectifiers
    1. Three Phase Semi Converter
    2. Three Phase Full Converter
    3. Three Phase Dual Converter

1. Semiconductors:

It is a single quadrant converter and it has only one polarity of output voltage and output current.

2. Full converter:

It is a two quadrant converter and the polarity of output voltage is either positive or negative but output current only a positive polarity.

3. Dual converter:

Dual converter operates in four quadrants therefore both the output voltage and output current can be either positive or negative.

Note: AC voltage controller uses the either ON-OFF control or phase angle control to transfer the power from source to load.

ON-OFF control:

In ON-OFF control SCR conducts fully for few cycles and fully OFF for some cycles. Therefore power is supplied from source to the load intermittently.

Phase angle control:

In phase angle control SCR’s are triggered in each half cycle of the supply with some delay. This delay is such that it transfers the required power from source to the load.

Advantages of circulating current mode of operation:

  • Circulating current maintains the continuous conduction of both converters over the entire control range independent of load.
  • Since one converter always operates as rectifier and another converter operates as inverter. The power flow is in either direction at any time is possible.
  • Since both the converters are continuous conduction over the time response for changing from one quadrant operation to another quadrant operation is fast.

Note: single phase converter has two modes of operation.

1. Converting mode (α<900):

The range of firing angle is 00<α<900. In this mode power flows from source to load.

2) Inverting mode (α >900):

The range of firing angle is 900 ≤ α < 1800. In this mode power flows from load to source.

Choppers (DC to DC converters):

To conversion of fixed input DC voltage into fixed or variable DC voltage output called choppers. Hence it also called as DC to DC converter.

  • Figure shows the block diagram of choppers is as shown in figure.
  • The output voltage VO or greater or less than the input voltage hence the coppers can be step down or step up type.
  • Chopper is basically a switch which connects and disconnects an input supply to the load for certain interval of time.
  • Choppers uses the switching devices like SCR, power BJT, MOSFET, IGBT, GTO etc.

1) Set up chopper: Conversion from lower input voltage to higher output voltage.

\[{V_O} > {V_i}\]

2) Step down chopper: conversion from higher input voltage to lower output voltage.

\[{V_O} < {V_i}\]

Classification of choppers:

Choppers are classified based on its operation in different quadrant such as single quadrant, two quadrant and four quadrant.

Types of choppers are,

  1. Class A
  2. Class B
  3. Class C
  4. Class D
  5. Class E

1) Class A Chopper:

  • This chopper operates in 1st quadrant. The output voltage VO and load current IO is always positive.
  • In this chopper current flows from source to load and energy (power) transferred from source to load. Hence this type of choppers operates as rectifier. Hence it is called forward motoring.

2) Class B Chopper:

  • This chopper operates in 2nd quadrant. The output voltage V0 is positive and load current I0 is negative.
  • In this chopper current flow from load to source and energy is transferred from load to source. This type of choppers operates as inverter. In this situation breaking operation of motors occurs hence it is called forward regenerative.
  • In this chopper output voltage V0 is greater than input voltage Vi. i.e., Vi0 hence it is called step up chopper.

3) Class C Chopper:

  • This chopper operates in two quadrants. It is combination of class A and class B choppers. It operates as a rectifier as well as inverter. In 1st quadrant forward motoring takes place and in 2nd quadrant forward regenerative breaking takes place.
  • Here output voltage is positive and output current is both positive and negative.
  • Here energy transferred in either direction that is source to load and load to source.

4) Class D Chopper:

  • This chopper operates in two quadrants (1st and 4th). The output current IO is positive and output voltage VO is positive or negative.
  • In 1st quadrant rectifying operation takes place hence it is called forward motoring. Here both VO and IO are positive.
  • When voltage is reversed VO is negative and IO is positive. Therefore inverting operation takes place that is the energy fed from load to source. Hence 4th quadrant operation is also called as reverse regeneration.

5) Class E Chopper:

  • This chopper operates in four quadrants. Output voltage as well as output current both can tak either positive or negative values.
  • 1st quadrant is forward motoring here VO and IO both are positive.
  • The 3rd quadrant is reverse motoring that is motor rotates in opposite direction compare to 1st quadrant. Since power flows from source to load in 3rd quadrant and it is rectifying operation.
  • In 2nd and 4th quadrant power flows from load to source hence it is called inverting operation.

Inverters (DC to AC converters):

The device used for conversion of DC power to AC power is called inverters. This process is called inversion. It is the reverse process of rectification.

Input is fixed or variable DC and output is variable voltage and variable frequency AC. Figure shows the principle of inverter.

  • The output voltage can be controlled with the help of drives of the switches.
  • The output voltage waveform of inverter can be square wave, quasi wave to get the sinusoidal output voltage by using the suitable techniques and design.

Classification of inverters:

  1. Based on output phases.
    1. Single phase inverter
    2. Three phase inverter
  2. Based on method of commutation.
    1. Line commutation inverters
    2. Forced commutation inverters
  3. Based on load connection or method of connecting thyristor and commutation element (based on switch connection).
    1. Series inverter
    2. Parallel inverter
    3. Bridge inverter
      1. Half bridge inverter
      2. Full bridge inverter
  4. Based on method control of nature of DC source feeding to inverter.
    1. Voltage source inverter (VSI) or voltage fed inverters (VFI)
    2. Current source inverter (CSI) or current fed inverter (CFI)

Two main types of inverters are,

1) Voltage source inverter (VSI):

Figure shows the block diagram of VSI. It is a constant voltage source. It can be obtained by connected a large capacitor across the DC voltage source for such inverters the amplitude of load voltage (VL) is equal to DC input voltage (VS). Here the current waveform depends upon the load.

2) Current source inverter (CSI):

Figure shows the block diagram of CSI. The input to the CSI is constant DC current source. This constant current source can be obtained by connecting a large inductor L in series with DC current source. Here the load current is equal to input current but output voltage depends upon the load.

Differences between VSI and CSI:

VSI CSI
1. Input is constant voltage 1. Input is constant current.
2. Current waveform depends on load. 2. Voltage waveform depends on load.
3. Large capacitor is connected across the DC voltage source of inverter. 3. Large inductor is connected in series with DC input current source of inverter.
4. Freewheeling diodes are required in case of inductive load. 4. Freewheeling diodes are not required.
5. Short circuit can damage the circuit. 5. Short circuit cannot damage the circuit.
6. A series resonance or tank circuit is required. 6. No resonance circuit is required.

Cyclo converters:

Cyclo converter is a circuit which converts the input AC of one frequency level to an output AC of another frequency level.

Types of cyclo converter:

Depending upon the output frequency the cyclo converters are mainly classified as,

1) Step up cyclo converter:

The frequency of the output is higher than the frequency if input then it is called step up cyclo converter.

\[{f_O} > {f_{in}}\]

2) Step down cyclo converter:

The frequency of output is lesser than the frequency of input then it is called step down cyclo converter.

\[{f_O} < {f_{in}}\]

Cyclo converters are also classified as,

  1. Single phase to single phase devices
    1. Mid-point configuration or center tapped configuration.
    2. Bridge configuration
  2. Three phase to single phase devices
  3. Three phase to three phase devices

Advantages of cyclo converters:

  • Cyclo converters are AC to AC converters without any DC link is used as in case of inverters.
  • Output frequency can be increased or decreased.
  • Conversion from single phase to single phase, three phase to single phase and three phase to three phase is possible.
  • It works well for even inductive load.
  • Cyclo converters are capable of supplying high powers.

Disadvantages of cyclo converters:

  • Output voltage of cyclo converter is not sinusoidal because it contains large no. of harmonics at high frequencies that is waveform is distraction.
  • Harmonics content is large hence heavy filters are required to remove the harmonics.
  • Cyclo converter requires several thyristors hence control system is complex and cost is also more.

  • In the half cycle T1 is triggered and conducts from 0 to t1 hence output voltage is positive. At time t1, T1 is forced commutated and T4 is triggered hence load voltage is negative. At time t2, T4 is forced commutated and T1 is triggered again therefore output voltage is again positive. At time t3, T1 is forced commutated and T4 is turned ON hence output voltage is negative.
  • At π, T4 is OFF and T3 is turned ON. Therefore output voltage is positive. At time t4, T3 is forced commutated and T2 is triggered hence output voltage is negative. The sequence is continuous.
  • Observe that output voltage waveform as the frequency of
    • \[{f_O} = \frac{1}{{{t_2} - {t_1}}}\]

    This frequency is higher than the input frequency.

Step down cyclo converter:

  • When the output frequency is less than the input frequency the converter can be operated as step down cyclo converter.
