CHAPTER 04 : ELECTRIC HEATING AND WELDING

Electrical Heating:

Electrical heating is a process in which electrical energy is converted into heat energy. It works on the principle of joule’s law.

Joule’s Law:

The amount of heat production in a conductor is directly proportional to the square of electrical current flowing through the conductor, Resistance of the conductor and the time for which electric current flows through the conductor.

\[H = {I^2}Rt\;\;\;\;\;Joules\]

Applications of electrical heating:

Now a days electrical heating is most used for domestic appliances as well as in industrial process control applications such as,

  • Electric iron
  • Electric oven
  • Electric toaster
  • Electric stove
  • Hotplates for cooking

Advantages of electrical heating:

Cleanliness: Electric energy is the cleanest form of energy it will not produce ash or dust during heating.

No pollution: Absence of flue, gases doesn’t results in pollution of atmosphere.

Ease of control: Simple, reliable and accurate control of temperature can be obtained.

Higher efficiency: The efficiency of utilization of electrical heating is much higher as compare to other system of heating.

Safety: Automatic protection against over current as overheating can be provided through proper switchgear.

Economical: Electrical energy is cheaper and it is produced in a large scale. The initial cost, running cost and maintenance cost of electrical energy are low.

Uniform heating: The heating can be generated with the work piece resulting in the uniform heating.

High temperature: The material can be heated to any temperature without any limit.

Heating of insulating materials: Insulating materials such as wood, plastic etc. can be heated by using dielectric heating method.

Low attention and maintenance cost: The attention required by electric heating equipment is negligible their by the maintenance cost is low.

Modes or methods of heat transfer:

There are three modes of heat transfer from a body at high temperature to a bod at low temperature. They are,

  1. Conduction
  2. Convection
  3. Radiation

1) Conduction:

In this mode of heat transfer the heat is transferred from one part of the substance to the other from molecular to molecular due to the temperature gradient between the two parts without the actual movement of the molecules.

Heat transferred in solids by conduction.

2) Convection:

In this method heat is transferred from one part of the substance to the other by actual movement of the heated molecules. Heat in water and air is transferred by convection.

3) Radiation:

In this mode of heat transfer, the heat is transferred from one body to the other without actually heating the medium in between. The sun rays reaches the earth without heating the atmosphere or a man sitting by the side of fire receives heat without the air in between getting heated up.

Direct resistance heating:

The above figure shows the diagram of direct resistance heating method. The material or charge to be heated is in the form of powder or small pieces or a liquid solution. The current is directly passed through the charge through electrodes. The charge itself forms the resistance and heat is directly produced in the charge. Where the supply is DC or AC single phase, two electrodes are used. If supply is three phase AC then three electrodes are used.

The electrodes are partially inversed on the charge and supply is given. When the charge is in the form of pieces, a powder of high resistivity material is sprinted over the surface of charge to prevent any possible short circuit between the pieces. This method of heating has high efficiency as heat is directly produced in the charge and in this method uniform and high temperature can be obtained.

Indirect resistance heating:

The above figure shows the diagram of indirect resistance heating. In this method the current is passed through resistive element which is either placed above or bellows the oven depending upon the nature of the job to be performed. The heat proportional to I2R losses produced in heating element deliver to the charge either by radiation or by convection. Both AC and DC supply can be used. Commonly ised resistance oven is a best example of this type automatic temperature control can be provided in this case.

Properties for making heating element:

1) High resistivity:

The resistivity if material used for making heating element should be high. This will reduces the length if the heating element.

2) Free from oxidization:

The element material should not be oxidized when subjected to high temperature.

3) High melting point:

Its melting point should be higher than its operating temperature. Otherwise a small rise in the voltage will damage the element.

4) Anticorrosive:

In many heating process the heating atmosphere is corrosive. When exposed to this atmosphere or any other chemical fumes the filament should not corrode. Otherwise its resistance will change and life will be shortened.

5) Mechanical Strength:

The material used should have sufficient mechanical strength of its own.

6) Withstand vibrations:

In most industrial processes quite strong vibrations are produced so the element material should withstand the vibrations while hot and should not breakdown.

7) Ductile:

To have convenient shapes and sizes the material used should have ductility and flexibility.

8) Low temperature coefficient of resistance:

For accurate temperature control the resistance should be nearly constant at all temperatures. This can be obtained only if the resistance of the material does not change appreciably.

Causes of failure of heating elements:

Some of the factors responsible for failure of heating elements are,

  1. Formation of hotspots
  2. Oxidization
  3. Corrosion
  4. Mechanical failure

Corrosion:

For industrial operations, if the filaments are directly exposed to chemical fumes the reactions will corrode the contact region; from this the passion of the element will ultimately fail.

Oxidization:

The outer or exposed surface the element gets oxidized at higher temperatures. During switching operations the oxide layer gets flacked off exposing inner surface to atmosphere. During further operation the exposed inner surface oxidized and resulting into formation of hotspots and ultimately breakdown.

Formation of hotspots:

Due to high rate of local osculation the resistance of the area increases such spots are called as hotspots. Due to generation of more heat at hotspots the heating element may get damaged. This indicated comparatively higher operating temperature at the particular spots as compare to the rest of the filament.

Causes of formation of hotspots are,

  1. Excessive oxidization
  2. Shielded by supporting structure
  3. Unequal spacing

Methods of controlling the temperature of resistance oven:

The temperature in a resistance furnace can be controlled using following methods.

  1. ON/OFF control (intermittent switching)
  2. Varying the no. of elements
  3. Variation in circuit configuration
  4. Change of applied voltage
    1. Tapping the input
    2. Bucking boosting the secondary voltage
    3. Auto transformer control
    4. Series reactor control

1. ON/OFF control (Intermittent Switching):

In this method the furnace voltage is switched ON/OFF intermittently. When the supply is switched OFF, heat produced is decreases and the temperature is reduced. When the supply is switched ON heat produced is increases and hence the temperature also increases. This type is suitable for small furnace only. Since entire furnace is switched OFF and ONN in this process, there is a lot of arching at the switching contacts.

2) Varying the no. of elements:

In this case with the help of a switch the no. of heating elements across the supply are changed, less no. of heating elements used, less will be the heat produced and hence the temperature will be less. In this case of three phase circuit equal no. of heating elements will be simultaneously switched of in each phase to maintain balanced condition. The most important drawback of varying the no. of elements is that it does not provide a uniform heating.

3) Variation in circuit configuration:

In this method the elements are connected in all possible series and parallel combinations and in star or delta by means of switches to obtain different temperatures. This is simplest and most commonly used method of temperature control. The different methods of connections are shown in figure.

In this method transformer having high voltage on primary side and tapping control also high voltage side is advisable.

5) Bucking and boosting the secondary voltage:

  • • In this method secondary winding is divided into two sections. One with lesser number of turns and other with larger number of turns.
  • • Depending on the direction of smallest section and current in the secondary winding, the induced EMF can be increased (boosting) or decreased (bucking).
  • If the secondary winding is connected in series and the direction of current in the smaller section is same. Its induced EMF adds to the EMF in the larger section of the secondary. This action is called as boosting. That is E2+E3.
  • When the connection of this secondary winding is reversed, the induced EMF in the smaller section is opposite to the larger section EMF. This action is called as bucking. That is E2-E3.

6) Series reactor control:

In this method a heavy coil wound on a core is placed in series with the furnace. A part of applied voltage draws across the reactor and rest appears across the furnace, thereby reducing the temperature inside the furnace.

The introduction of reactance in series with the furnace can reduces the PF. A capacitor is introduced in the circuit for keeping the PF near unity.

7) Auto transformer control:

In this method an auto transformer with tapping is used as shown in the figure. It is used to obtain different voltages across the furnace and hence temperature can be controlled.

Arc Furnace (Arc Heating):

The arc furnace is used for melting or extracting of metals need a high temperature operation.

If an air gap between two electrode is subjected to a very high voltage, the air in between gets ionized and the ionized air becomes conducting. Hence the current starts flowing between the two electrodes through the air in the form of continuous spark (arc). This electric discharge between two electrodes through air is known as an electric arc. The temperature obtained from the arc is between 30000 C and 35000 C.

Direct Arc Heating:

In this type the charge act as another electrode, so that the arc current flows through the charge and there is a direct contact between the arc and the charge. Electrically these furnaces are further sub divided into 2 categories.

  1. Conducting bottom
  2. Non-conducting bottom

The heating process is faster in direct arc furnace hence heating efficiency is too high.

Direct arc furnace is usually adapted for steel production due to its high capital cost it is usually not employed for melting process.

Indirect Arc Heating:

In this method arc is produced between two electrodes and heat is transmitted to the charge by radiation. That is the arc does not come in contact with the charge but heat is transferred through radiation from arc to top layer of charge and through conduction from top layer to the bottom layer in the charge. The construction of such type of furnace limits the use of two electrodes, there by only single phase is used. The current does not flow through the charge, hence there is no automatic stirring and furnace is require to be rocked mechanically.

Induction heating:

An induction furnace is an electrical furnace in which heat is applied by induction heating of metal. Induction heating is based on the principle of AC transformer. There is a primary winding through which an AC current is passed this coil is magnetically coupled with the metal to be heated. An electric current is induced in this metal is dependent on,

  1. The magnitude of the primary current.
  2. The ratio of no. of turns in the primary and secondary circuit.
  3. The coefficient of magnetic coupling.

This induced current has the heating effect similar to any other electric current through an ordinary resistance.

Direct core type induction furnace:

In this type the charge forms the short circuited secondary of the transformer. Therefore it is like a transformer. The secondary is magnetically coupled to the primary winding. The charge is melted due to heavy current induced in it. When there is no molten metal no current will be flowing in the secondary. Thus to start the furnace molten is poured in the annular hearth or a sufficient quantity is left over from the previous melted charge.

Indirect core type induction furnace:

In this type of furnace the principle of induction has been utilized for providing heat treatment to metal. Figure represents the indirect induction heating. The secondary winding forms the walls of metal container and the iron core links the primary as well as secondary winding. Heat produced due to induced current is transmitted to the charge by radiation. The part LM of the magnetic circuit is made up a special alloy and it’s kept inside the chamber of furnace. When the temperature reaches its critical value the reluctance increases appreciably there by decreasing the inductive effect thus the temperature of the furnace can be very effectively control which is a great advantage.

High frequency coreless induction heating:

The eddy currents developed in any magnetic circuits are given as,

\[EddyCurrent = \alpha {\beta ^2}{f^2}\]

Where β flux density and f is is the frequency

In a coreless furnace there is no core and thus the flux density will be low. Hence for compensating the low flux density the primary current applied to the primary coil should have sufficient high frequency. Thus by applying current of high frequency the core of the induction furnace can be eliminated their by reducing its weight and increasing the flexibility. The above figure shows the general construction of high frequency coreless induction furnace. The eddy currents are sufficient to heat the metal to melting point and also setup electromagnetic forces.

Due to high frequency there will be skin effect and it causes the resistance of the conductor to increase slightly in the primary coil and it will cause high copper losses. Due to high amount of copper losses the temperature of primary winding increases considerably. To cool down the coil artificial method is employed.

Advantages of coreless induction furnace:

  • Operation cost is low.
  • Construction cost of a coreless furnace is low.
  • There is no dust, smoke and noise etc.
  • The time taken to reach the melting temperature is less.
  • Precise control of power in to the charge can be employed.
  • Simple charging and pouring.
  • Intermittent use.

Disadvantages of coreless induction furnace:

  • As the frequency increases it leads to skin effect.

Applications:

Surface hardening of steel parts: The passion of steel component to be hardened is covered with the induction coil.

Brazing: Two pieces to be soldered with non-conventional soldering compound like silver, copper alloys can be joined together by placing the soldering alloy between suitably flux created surfaces.

Annealing: Brass, bronze etc. can be annealed efficiently without having a brighter surface if the metal is preheated to a specific temperature.

Butt welding: Moderate sized members can be butt welded by momentary induction heating of the ends to be welded together.

Sterilization of medical instrument: Heating of some instruments to a certain temperature for a specific time makes the instruments germ free.

High frequency eddy current heating:

High frequency eddy current heating is nothing but a form as induction heating, usually employed for hardening, annealing and tempering of machine parts. The machine part to be heated is surrounded by a coil through which an alternating current at high frequency is passed. The electromagnetic field developed in the coil produces heating effect in the desired area of the machine parts or metals.

The heating effect is due to eddy currents setup in the machine parts. The usual range for which the high frequency or eddy current heating is selected lays between 10 thousand cycles per sec to 40 thousand cycles per sec.

High frequency eddy current heating can be shown that the depth of penetration that is depth at which the current density drops to some value at the surface is given by,

\[d = \frac{1}{{2\pi }}\sqrt {\frac{{\rho \times {{10}^9}}}{{{\eta _r}f}}} cm\]

Where,

d= depth of penetration in cm
ρ= resistivity
ηr= relative permeability of the material
f= frequency

Advantages:

  • The amount of heat produced can also be control by using lining devices.
  • The heat produced can be penetrated to any desired depth by the selection of frequency.
  • Unskilled labor can also operate the equipment.

Disadvantages:

  • The generation of heat is costly.
  • The efficiency of the operation is poor.

Dielectric heating:

Dielectric heating is also called as high frequency capacitive heating and is used for heating insulating materials such as wood, plastic, glass, ceramics etc. In dielectric heating, uniform heating of insulating materials is possible which is not possible by other methods. The normal voltage used varies from 600 V to 3000 V but however voltages upto 20 KV can be used. The supply frequency required is 10 to 50 MHz. the overall efficiency is about 50%.

Advantages:

  • Heating is uniform and is produced in the material itself.
  • The rate of heat produced may be increased by increasing frequency and voltage of the supply.
  • It is the only method of heating insulating materials.
  • Heating can be stopped immediately whenever desired.
  • During heating, no flames are produced and hence very convenient for heating plastics, wood etc.

Disadvantages:

  • The only disadvantage is that, cost of heating is very high.

Applications:

• Gluing of wood: For gluing of wood sheets or boards dielectric heating is most commonly used.

Food processing: Cooking of foods without removing the outer shells. Dehydration of fruits, milk, eggs, creams etc. Pasteurizing of milk, bear etc. inside bottles.

Diathermy: Dielectric heating is used for heating of tissues and bones of human body for querying certain types of pains and deceases.

Pre heating of plastics:

Baking of foundry cores: In foundries, resin type thermo setting binders are used as they set almost instantaneously when brought to poly migration temperature.

Sterilization: Dielectric heating can be used to sterilize gauges, instruments, bandages etc. during treatment of patients.

Dielectric heating is used for drying purpose in textile industries and removal of moisture from oil emulsions.

Principle of dielectric heating:

The principle of operation of dielectric heating is that when a capacitor is subjected to a sinusoidal voltage, the current drawn by it is never leading the voltage exactly by 900. The angle between voltage and current is slightly less than 900 called as the loss angle.

Electrical welding:

The welding is a process of joining two or more metals together.

The electric welding is a branch of welding in which the electric current is used to produce required amount of heat for joining two or more metals together.

Types of electrical welding:

  1. Resistance welding
    1. Butt welding
    2. Spot welding
    3. Flash welding
    4. Projection welding
    5. Seam welding
  2. Arc welding
    1. Metal arc welding
    2. Carbon arc welding
    3. Helium or organ arc welding
    4. Atomic hydrogen arc welding

Electrical resistance welding:

The amount of heat required to joining the metal by passing heavy current through the material to be welded by applying sufficient mechanical pressure.

The heat generated by passing an electrical current through resistance of R in ohm.

\[H = {I^2}Rt\;\;\;\;\;Joules\]

Butt welding:

In this type of butt welding the material parts to be welded or joined end to end as shown in figure. The heating in this particular case is obtained by the contact resistance of the metals to be welded. A pressure is applied either manually or with toggle mechanism. A heavy current is passed from the welding transformer which creates the necessary heat at the joined due to the high resistance of the contact area. The metal at the join melts the parts fuse together providing a bulged joint.

Flash welding:

This is similar to butt welding except in this method the voltage to the metal part is applied before these are butted together. The two metal pieces to be welded are clamped strongly in a flash welding machine. The metal parts when come near to each other there them appearance on arc which burns the contact surface. This arc continuous and burns away some part of the material. As soon as the welding temperature reached the power supply is cutoff.

Spot welding:

Fig (a): Single spot.

Fig (b): Multiple spot.

Spot welding is performed by sending an electrical current through two pieces of metal sheets creating a weld at this spot where the electricity was introduced.

The metal sheets are placed in between two water cooled electrodes, which must possess high electrical and thermal conductivity. The water cooled electrode is pumped water to cool the metal sheets after the weld is completed. The electrical current used in spot welding moves through the electrode at a rate of mili seconds and produces limited amount of heat therefore making it less dangerous and required less energy to perform.

Seam welding:

It is similar to spot welding except that series of spots are produced by roller electrodes instead of tip electrodes. The electrodes will localize the current and pressure to thee welding point. The wheels in this case are forming two electrodes, either the wheels are moved on the metal sheets or the plates are moved under wheels. An interrupt is provider on the circuit which turns ON the supply for a period sufficient to hot the welding point. After the point welded the current is interrupted by an interrupter. Water solution is used to cool the metals after the weld is completed.

When there are two plates of different cross sectional areas, then it is necessary for obtaining desirable strength to have the projection on the ticker place. In case the welding is required on plates of different materials then the projection should be made on the high conductivity materials.

Electric arc welding:

In this method an arc is obtained between the electrode and the metal connected to a suitable source. Required amount of current can obtained either AC or DC source. One terminal is connected to the electrode and other to the work piece and the circuit is completed through an air gap is provided between the tip of the electrode and the surface of the work piece by leaping the electrode at a distance of about 3 mm to 6 mm from the surface of work piece. Due to interruption by the air gap is produced and the temperature attained 35000C and the electrical energy is converted into the arc and into heat energy.

For obtaining arc the required amount of voltage on AC supply system is between 70 to 100 V and on DC supply system is between 50 V to 60 V. once the arc is struck, 20-30 V is only required to maintain heat.

AC welding transformer with reactance coil:

In this case AC machine (transformer) having drooping characteristics is used. The drooping characteristic is obtained in AC equipment by means of reactance coil in series with the arc. The normal supply voltage is stepped down to the striking voltage. The voltage at the secondary side of the transformer remains constant almost and it available for striking the arc, but as soon as the current flow the voltage drop across the reactance coil reduces the voltage at the arc to the required value. The reactance coil also controls the flow of current. This is archived by either having tapped coils or by the lamination iron core of the reactance coil.

Laser beam welding:

Laser beam welding is a modern welding process. The focal spot is targeted on the work piece surface which will be welded. At the surface the large concentration of light energy is converted into thermal energy. The surface of the work piece starts melting and progresses through it by surface conductance. The penetration of the work piece depends on conducted heat and the thickness of the material to be welded. The losing source is based on the principle of excitation of atoms using intense light, electron beams, chemicals and the spontaneous release of photons. The role of focusing lenses in this process is really important because it concentrates the beam energy into a focal point.
