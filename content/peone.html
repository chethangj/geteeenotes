Power Diode:

Fig (a): Symbol

Fig (b): Structure

Power diodes are required in most of the power converters. Power diode is a unidirectional device.

When anode A is positive with respect to cathode (k) diode starts conducting, current flows from anode to cathode and diode set to be forward bias.

When cathode voltage is greater than anode voltage than the diode do not conduct and is set to be reverse bias which is shown in above figure a.

Structure of Power Diode:

Figure b shows the structure of power diode.

  • The n+ subtract forms the cathode of the diode.
  • The pn junction is formed by diffusing a heavily doped p+ region. This p+ region forms anode of the diode.
  • The thickness of n- drift region depends upon the breakdown voltage of the diode.
  • For higher breakdown voltage drift region is wide and almost absent for low power signal diode.
  • This power diode conducts only in forward bias condition that is from anode to cathode.

VI Characteristics of Power Diode:

  • When the positive of the supply is given to anode and negative of the supply is given to cathode then the diode is set to be forward bias. In forward bias condition anode current increases linearly with voltage.
  • The negative of the supply is connected to the anode and positive of the supply is connected to the cathode then the supply is connected to the cathode then the diode is set to reverse biased. When diode is reverse biased very small anode current flows called as leakage current.
  • When the voltage exceeds VRBD, anode current rises rapidly hence large power dissipation takes place in the diode which may damage the device. This can be avoided by operating the diode bellow the breakdown voltage.

Applications of Power Diode:

They are used in rectifiers, voltage amplifiers, battery chargers, chopper, inverters, and converters.

Silicon Control Rectifier (SCR):

Fig (a): Symbol

Fig (b): Structure

SCR is a four layer; three terminal semiconductor device which is shown is above figure.

Structure:

It consists of three pn junction namely J1, J2 and J3. Anode (A) is taken out from the P1 layer and cathode (K) is taken out from the N2 layer and gate (G) is taken out from the P2 layer. The gate terminal is used to turn ON the device which is shown in the above figure.

SCR is having three basic modes of operation.

  1. Forward blocking mode (FBM)
  2. Forward conduction mode (FCM)
  3. Reverse blocking mode (RBM)

Working of SCR:

(i) Forward Biased:

  • When anode is positive with respect to cathode SCR is set to be forward bias which is shown in fig (a).
  • During forward blocking mode, Ig=0 that is gate is kept open, under this condition junction J1 and J3 is forward bias J2 is reverse bias. Therefore SCR will not conduct when a small voltage is applied.
  • But if applied voltage (VAK) is increased to a certain value called forward break over voltage (VBO) then the junction J2 is breakdown. This causes SCR to conduct hence called as forward conduction mode even when gate is kept open.
  • In ON state the current through the SCR is large and it can be controlled by the applied voltage and external resistance.

Fig (a): Forward Bias of SCR

(ii) Reverse Bias:

When anode is made negative with respect to cathode SCR is set to be reverse bias. In this mode junction J1 and J3 is reverse bias and J2 is forward bias. There is no current in the gate terminal expect reverse leakage current flow to the SCR. Hence SCR will be in the OFF state. This mode is called as reverse blocking mode shown in fig (b).

If reverse voltage VAK is increases to a certain value called reverse break over voltage (VRO) then a large current will start to flow in SCR which may damage SCR.

Fig (b): Reverse Bias of SCR

Latching Current (IL):

It is the minimum anode current required maintaining the SCR in ON state immediately after a thyristor has been turned ON, gate signal has been removed.

“The ability of SCR to remain conducting even when gate signal is removed is known as latching current.” The region C is in forward conduction mode.

Holding Current (IH):

It is the minimum anode current to maintain the SCR in ON state but the holding current is less than the latching current i.e., IH less than IL, when anode current reduces bellow the holding current SCR turns OFF.

Reverse Biased Characteristics:

A very small current flows to the SCR due to minority charge carriers. When the reverse voltage reaches reverse break over voltage SCR conducts called reverse blocking mode but the device gate is damaged.

Two Transistor Model of SCR (or) Two Transistor Analogy:

Fig (a): Equivalent Circuit

Fig (b): Basic Structure

Construction:

A thyristor can be considered as two complementary transistors one is pnp (Q1) and another is npn (Q2). The base of the transistor Q1 is connected to the collector of Q2. Similarly base of the transistor Q2 is connected to the collector of Q1. In this circuit we have an action of positive feedback or regeneration. It means that if there is any change in current at any point in the loop formed by transistor Q1 and Q2 it is amplified and return to the starting point with the same phase.

Working:

Fig a and b shows the equivalent circuit and basic structure of two transistor model of SCR. If the base current of transistor Q2 increases the collector current of transistor Q2 is also increased. It causes more base current through the transistor Q1 due to which collector current of Q1 also increases. This action will continue till both the transistors are driven into the saturation. In this circuit SCR act as ON switch and it will pass current from anode to cathode.

Applications of SCR:

  • SCR’s best suitable for uncontrolled rectifiers.
  • It is used in AC regulators, light dimmers, heating applications.
  • DC motor drives, large power supplies and electronic circuit breaker.
  • Battery charger.
  • Inverters.

DIAC:

Fig (a): Symbol

Fig (b): Structure

Fig (c): VI Characteristics of DIAC

A DIAC is a two terminal bidirectional triggering device which can be switched from OFF state to the ON state during both the cycle (forward and reverse bias (or) positive and negative cycle) of AC input signal. This device consists of 2 PNPN section switches connected in antiparallel which is shown in fig (b) but it has no gate terminal. DIAC is similar to 2 SCR’s connected in antiparallel without a gate terminal. It is also called as trigger diode. The symbol is shown in fig (a).

Operation of DIAC:

The section P1N1P2N2 has junctions J2, J3, J4 similarly the section P2N1P1N3 as junction J1, J2, J3.

When MT1 is positive and MT2 is negative and the voltage is increases and the DIAC turns ON which offers negative resistance and current flows from MT1 to MT2.

Similarly MT2 is made positive and MT1 is made negative when voltage is increases the DIAC turns ON which offers negative resistance and current flows from MT2 to MT1.

When the applied voltage is less than VBO or VBR the DIAC blocks the flow of current and ac as open switch that is device is in OFF state.

When the applied voltage exceeds VBO or VBR the DIAC conducts and the current start flowing from MT1 to MT2 or MT2 to MT1 at the device set to be ON state.

Applications of DIAC:

  • They are widely used in AC control circuit such as lamp dimmers, heat control, motor speed control etc.
  • Trigger devices for SCR control applications.

Light Activated SCR (LASCR) or Photo Diode:

Fig (a): Symbol

Fig (b): Structure

Fig (c): VI Characteristics of LASCR

The light activated SCR can be triggered using a beam of light. Its gate region is photo sensitive. Figure (a) shows the symbol and figure (b) shows the VI characteristics of LASCR.

It has four layer 3 junctions just like an SCR. There is no gate terminal. Figure (b) shows the structure of LASCR. The layer P1 is exposed to sun light triggers the LASCR.

When light is incident in P1 region, electron hole pairs diffuse towards P1N1 that is junction J3. Now junctions J1 J3 is forward biased and J2 is reverse biased hence more light is required to break down the junction J2. If the junction J2 is broken then the current across J2 increases which drops voltage across anode and cathode this makes LASCR to turn ON.

LASCR turns ON even at low anode to cathode voltage if incident light intensity is higher.

Advantages of LASCR:

  • It can be turned ON by a beam of light.
  • Low power losses in ON state.
  • Turn ON time is short.

Applications:

  • It is used in high power applications like HVDC transmission.
  • Used in noise environments for better triggering control.

Power Transistor:

It is broadly classified into,

  1. Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT)
  2. Metal Oxide Semiconductor Field Effect Transistor (MOSFET)
  3. Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistor (IGBT)

1. BJT:

Fig: NPN & PNP BJT

In NPN BJT when the base emitter junction is forward bias the transistor turns ON and current flows from collector to emitter. The BJT turns OFF as soon as base emitter drive is removed.

In PNP BJT the base emitter junction is forward bias the transistor turns ON and current flows from emitter to collector. The BJT turns OFF as soon as base emitter drive is removed.

Power transistor or BJT is a bipolar device. There are two types NPN and PNP.

The available current and voltage ratings are higher for NPN devices.

VI Characteristics of BJT:

Figure shows the VI characteristics of BJT. These characteristics are also called output characteristics. The collector current IC is plotted with respect to collector emitter voltage for different values of base current. There are four region namely cutoff region, active region, quasi saturation and hard saturation.

BJT as a switch:

In On state BJT operates in quasi or hard saturation region. In OFF state BJT operates in cutoff region.

Cutoff region: The cutoff region is the area where base current is almost 0. Hence no current flows and transistor is OFF.

Quasi and Hard Saturation: In quasi and hard saturation when base drive is applied and transistor is set to be turn ON. Hence the collector current flows depending upon the load and also flows from collector to emitter.

Active Region: BJT is never operated in active region. In this region BJT acts as an amplifier.

Note: Transistor is current control device. The base current decides it is in ON state or OFF state. In transistor base is a control terminal.

Region EB Junction CB Junction State
Cutoff Region Reverse Bias Reverse Bias OFF
Active Region Forward Bias Reverse Bias Act as an Amplifier
Saturation Region Forward Bias Reverse Bias ON

Merits of BJT:

  • BJT have small turn ON and turn OFF time hence their switching frequencies are higher.
  • BJT have small turn ON losses.
  • Base drive has fully controlled over the operation of BJT.
  • BJT do not require commutation circuits.
  • BJT is a bipolar device.
  • BJT available at low cost.

Demerits of BJT:

  • Base drive circuit of BJT is complex.
  • Storage charge in base reduces the switching frequencies.

Application of BJT:

  • SMPS (Switched Mode Power Supply)
  • Bridge inverters
  • DC to DC converters (Choppers)
  • Power factor correction technics.

Operation of NPN transistor as a switch:

  • The transistor operates in regions namely active region, saturation region and cutoff region.
  • When transistor is made to operate in active region then it acts as an amplifier.
  • When it is made to operate in cutoff region and saturation region it acts as a switch. For power electronic applications transistor is mainly used as a switch. The saturation state occurs when both EB and CB junctions are forward biased. The cutoff state occurs when both the junctions are reversing biased. A transistor can be used as a electronic switch which can turn ON and turn OFF the current.
  • When input base voltage (VBE) is positive the transistor saturates the collector current IC will flows through the collector resistance RC. This condition similar to a closed switch (ON) and the LED will glow.
  • When input base voltage is negative the transistor operates in cutoff region and no collector current (IC) flows through the collector resistance RC. This condition is similar to that of open switch (OFF) and the LED does not glow.

Switching Characteristics of BJT:

Fig (a): Equivalent Model

Fig (b): Switching Waveforms of BJT

  • Figure (a) and (b) shows the equivalent model of BJT and switching waveforms of BJT. Base to emitter voltage (VBE) is positive to turn ON the BJT. When VBE is ON base current (IB) also start flowing.
  • In figure (b) shows that when base drive is applied collector current does not start flowing because collector base junction capacitance (CBE) state charging after the base drive is applied.
  • Delay time (td) time required for collector current start increasing after base drive is applied. Now CBE charges to forward bias voltage (0.7 V) the collector current reaches to its steady state value. Now the BJT is fully turned ON.
  • Rise time (t1) time required for collector current to reach its steady state value. Turn ON time of BJT is equal to the sum of delay time and rising time.
    • \[{t_{ON}} = {t_d} + {t_r}\]
  • For turn OFF of the transistor base voltage is negative hence base current also negative but the collector current does not change till the time ts called storage time. In this time saturating charge is removed from the base.
  • When storage charge is removed base current and collector current starts flowing and reaches to the zero (storage charge is removed because of negative base current).
  • Once storage charge is removed CBE start charges the negative base voltage but base current is zero. The fall of the collector current depends on negative charge storage in CBE. The time required to fall of collector current is called full time.
  • The turn of time of the transistor is equal to the sum of the storage time (ts) and full time (tf).
    • \[{t_{OFF}} = {t_s} + {t_f}\]

Note: Here storage time ts is more because more time is required to remove the extra charge of the base.

MOSFET:

MOSFET are the majority carrier device. Power MOSFET is a device derived from FET. It is used as fast acting switch at low power levels.

BJJ is a current control device whereas MOSFET is a voltage control device. This is a fast switching transistor suitable for high frequency and low power applications.

Power MOSFET’s are in two types.

  1. N-Channel MOSFET
  2. P-Channel MOSFET
  • MOSFET has three terminals. Gate (G), Drain (D) and Source (S).
  • When gate to source voltage is applied the MOSFET is turned ON. The current flows from drain to source.
  • When gate to source voltage is removed or zero the MOSFET is turned OFF. No current flows from drain to source.
  • Only voltage is applied to turn ON and turn OFF of a MOSFET. Thus gate has fully control over the conduction of MOSFET. Hence the MOSFET is a voltage control device.

There are two types of MOSFET’s. Depletion type and Enhancement type MOSFET.

  • Figure shows the structure of n channel enhancement type MOSFET.
  • The source and drain are connected to n+ regions which are heavily doped have intensity of 1019/cm3. P type body region forms the channel between drain and source having doping level intensity of 1015/cm3.
  • The gate metal is connected to the P type region in between an insulating gate oxide (SiO2) layer exists.
  • Figure (c) shows the four layer structure of N-Channel enhancement MOSFET. This is n+ p n- n+ structure. A drift region (n-) is lightly doped is shown in structure.
  • In this structure gate overlaps the P type and n+ region. Source also overlaps the P type and n+ region.
  • When gate to source (VGS) voltage is applied, accumulation layer of electrons is generated in the body region (P type) near oxide layer called induced channel of electrons.
  • Therefore the drain to source current (IDS) flows through this induced channel. When VGS is equal to zero, induced channel absent. Therefore no IDS current flows. This is called N-channel enhancement MOSFET.
  • Current IDS starts flowing from drain to source because of drift region ON state voltage drop of MOSFET increases thickness of drift region determines the breakdown voltage of MOSFET.
  • Here parasitic BJT is former base is in P type, emitter is in n+ and collector is in n- drift region. Emitter and base of the parasitic BJT are shorted to source, BJT does not conduct.

IGBT:

  • IGBT is a voltage controlled switch. It is obtained by combining the property of MOSFET and BJT. The gate circuit of MOSFET and collector emitter circuit of BJT is combining together to form a new device called IGBT. Hence IGBT have advantages of both BJT and MOSFET.
  • BJT have low ON state losses for high value of current but MOSFET can turned ON and OFF faster hence MOSFET have high ON state losses.
  • The drive circuit of BJT is complex but drive circuit of MOSFET is simple hence IGBT is formed.
  • IGBT has three terminals gate, collector and emitter. Therefore current flows from collector to emitter.
  • When gate to emitter voltage is applied IGBT is turned ON. When gate to emitter voltage is removed IGBT turned OFF. Thus gate has fully controlled over the conduction of IGBT.

Structure of IGBT:

  • The structure of IGBT is similar to that of MOSFET. Figure shows the vertical crossection of IGBT. In this structure n+ p n- n+ p+ regions are present.
  • Compare to MOSFET there is an additional p+ injecting layer is present. This layer is collector (drain) of IGBT. This p+ injecting layer is heavily doped having doping intensity of 1019/cm3.
  • The doping of other layer is similar to that of MOSFET. N+ layers have 1019/cm3, P type region has doping level of 1016/cm3.
  • N- drift region is lightly doped having doping level of 1014/cm3. This region results in ON state losses of IGBT increases. Thickness of drift region determines the breakdown voltage.

Applications of IGBT:

  • AC motor drive that is inverters.
  • DC to DC power supply that is choppers.
  • UPS systems.
  • Harmonic compensator.

Gate Turn OFF Thyristor (GTO):

  • GTO is called as gate turn OFF thyristor. It is similar to the SCR but it can be turned OFF by gate terminal/ figure shows the bidirectional current flows through the gate.
  • A small positive voltage is applied to gate to cathode of GTO a positive current flow through the gate of GTO then GTO is turned ON. Similarly GTO can be turned OFF by negative current flows through the gate. Hence gate has fully controlled over the conduction of GTO.
  • GTO do not requires any commutation circuit for turn OFF because it is turn OFF by applying negative gate current.
  • The operation of GTO can be explained with the help of two transistor analogy. The gain of the transistor is reduced. This reduces the regenerative action.

Applications of GTO:

  • GTO’s are suitable for low power applications.
  • Induction heating and motor drives.
  • Rolling mills, robotics and machine tools.
  • Adjustable frequency inverter drives.
  • Traction drive.
