CHAPTER 02 : SCR Control Circuits

Methods of turn ON the SCR:

1. High forward voltage triggering:

A small positive voltage is applied to the gate to cathode of SCR. The forward voltage VF or anode to cathode voltage VAK exceeds the forward break over voltage (VBO) then the SCR is turned ON.

\[{V_{AK}} > {V_{BO}}\]

(At these voltages the leakage current is so high the internal regeneration is starts in the device.)

2. dv/dt triggering or rate of change of voltage triggering:

The thyristor has an internal capacitor when it is in forward biased state. The rate of change of voltage is applied across the SCR becomes large because leakage current flows through the device increases which is due to use of internal capacitor. This results in SCR to be turned ON.

3. Exceeding the internal device temperature or thermal triggering:

Thyristor is turned ON due to increased temperature. At high temperature there are more electron hole pairs across the junctions. This increases the leakage current then thyristor is turned ON.

4. Focusing the light beam on the junction or photon triggering:

Thyristor can be turned ON by light. When light falls on gate to cathode junction of the thyristor, light induces the electron hole pairs at the junction. It helps to increases the leakage current. This results in turning ON of SCR.

5. Gate triggering or gate drive:

It is the most popular method of turn ON of SCR. In this method a small positive voltage is applied to the gate to cathode of SCR. A small current flow through the circuit the SCR is turned ON. Here gate current flows then anode to cathode current increases and regenerative action starts.

The triggering circuits are called as firing circuits.

  • For gate of SCR is triggered, we required the following blocks in the firing circuit.
  • The firing circuit should reduce the triggering pulses for every thyristor at approximate instants.
  • A regulated power supply can be used to feed the power to the main blocks of firing circuit.
  • When SCR is turned ON gate triggering pulses are required which is generated by the control circuit. Then it is amplified and passed to the gate of SCR through isolation circuit.
  • The triggering pulses generate by control circuit have very small power. This power is increased by a pulse amplifier.
  • The firing circuit operates at low voltage levels (5 to 20V) and thyristor operates at high voltage levels (>250V). Hence there must be a electrical isolation between firing circuit and thyristor. This isolation is provided by pulse transformer or optocoupler.

(ii). RC Half Wave Firing Circuit:

Fig (a): Circuit Diagram

Fig (b): Waveforms of RC Half Wave Firing Circuit

  • Figure shows the RC half wave firing circuit. It consist of thyristor T1, two diodes D1 and D2, capacitor C and variable resistance R. the wave form is shown above.
  • By using the variable resistor R we can control the firing angle of thyristor from 0 to 1800.
  • During the negative half cycle of supply voltage the capacitor charges through the diode D2.
  • During the positive half cycle of the supply voltage SCR is triggered at firing angle then the capacitor discharges through the resistor R.
  • When voltage across the capacitor (VC) reaches the value which is greater than minimum gate voltage (Vg(min) i.e., VC= Vg(min) the SCR is triggered and turns ON.
  • The diode D1 prevents the negative capacitor voltage which is appearing to the gate of the thyristor.
  • Triggering is controlled only in one half cycle of the supply voltage i.e., positive half cycle. Hence this circuit is called RC half wave firing circuit.

(iii). RC Full Wave Firing Circuit:

Fig (a): Circuit Diagram

Fig (b): Waveforms of RC Full Wave Firing Circuit

  • Figure shows the RC full wave firing circuit. It consists of thyristor T1 and four diodes D1, D2, D3 and D4. D1, D2 conducts and D3, D4 is OFF when it is in positive half cycle of supply voltage.
  • During the negative half cycle of supply voltage D3, D4 conducts and D1, D2 are OFF.
  • The supply to the thyristor is given through the uncontrolled rectifier. Hence both half cycles are positive cycles to the thyristor.
  • For every half cycle at the beginning of a cycle the capacitor starts charging. When capacitor voltage (VC) reaches to the value which is greater than minimum gate voltage, the thyristor is turned ON. VC≥Vg(min)
  • Once the thyristor is turned ON the capacitor voltage clamped (reduces) to zero that is VC=0 till the next half cycle exists.
  • The firing angle of thyristor is controlled by a variable resistor R varied from 0 to 1800.
  • The triggering is controlled in both the half cycle hence it is called full wave RC firing circuit.

UJT Pulse Triggering Circuit:

Fig (a): UJT Pulse Triggering Circuit

Fig (b): Waveforms of UJT Pulse Triggering Circuit

  • The AC supply voltage is rectified and it is given to the zener regulator. The zener diode (Z) clamps the rectified voltage to a constant voltage VZ. This voltage is applied to the UJT circuit acts as a supply voltage to UJT.
  • Here the pedestal control indicates the initial voltage level in the capacitor and it is adjusted by resistor R. the ramp control RC indicates the charging of the capacitor from pedestal level. The wave form is shown above.
  • The capacitor charges through resistor RC and discharges through emitter (E), base (B1) and primary of the pulse transformer.
  • When capacitor voltage (VC) is equal to the peak voltage of UJT (VP) UJT is turned ON and capacitor start discharges then pulse is generated.
  • The pulses are obtained from UJT are used to trigger the SCR.
  • The firing angle or delay angle of UJT is varied from 0 to 1800. Hence triggering pulses is generated in every half cycle.
  • When a capacitor discharge UJT is turned ON then UJT is turn OFF only when again the capacitor starts charging from pedestal level. (Initial voltage level of capacitor). This mode of working of UJT is called relaxation oscillator.

  • This process repeats for every half cycle of an input signal.

Commutation:

SCR is turned ON by applying gate pulse. Once the SCR is turned ON the gate has no control over the conduction of SCR by removing the gate pulse also and SCR can’t be turned OFF. So external commutation circuit is required to turn OFF the SCR.

Fig (a): Gate Pulse is Used to Turn ON SCR

Fig (b): External Commutation Circuit is Required to Turn OFF SCR

The process or the method of turning OFF the thyristor is called commutation.

External commutation circuit uses the LC components to turn OFF the SCR. The L and C store the energy when SCR is turn ON and then this stored energy is used to turn OFF the thyristor.

Commutations are in two types.

  1. Natural commutation
  2. Forced commutation

1. Natural Commutation:

Fig (a): Halfwave Rectifier Uses the Natural Commutation to Turn OFF

Fig (b): Waveforms of Halfwave Controlled Rectifier for Natural Commutation

  • The natural commutation does not need any external components. It uses the supply voltage for turning OFF the SCR. Hence it is also called as line commutation. Figure shows the half wave circuit using the natural commutation.
  • The input supply voltage is applied, during positive half cycle of the supply voltage SCR is triggered at firing angle since SCR is forward biased. It starts conducting and load current IO starts flowing through the load resistance R. therefore load is resistive output current is,
    • \[{I_O} = \frac{{{V_O}}}{R}\]
  • Here the shape of the output current is same as the output voltage but amplitude decreases and output current is same as SCR current.
  • At π supply voltage become zero hence current through SCR becomes zero. Therefore SCR turn OFF.
  • When supply voltage is reversed or zero the voltage appears across the SCR becomes zero and SCR is in reverse biased. It does not conduct.
  • Natural commutation take place only when input supply voltage is AC and it does not uses any external components for turn OFF SCR.

2. Forced Commutation:

  • • Forced commutation is used when supply voltage is DC. A commutation circuit is connected across the SCR is as shown in above figure.
  • • The commutation circuit is normally uses LC circuit. The LC circuit stores the energy when SCR is turned ON. This energy can be used to turn OFF the SCR.
  • • The LC circuit imposes (forces) reverse bias across the SCR due to stored energy. Hence forward current (IAK) of SCR is dropped below the holding current and SCR is turned OFF.
    • \[i.e.,\;{I_{AK}} < {I_H}\]

Comparison of natural and forced commutation:

Natural Commutation Forced Commutation
1. No external commutation components are require to turn OFF SCR. 1. External commutation components (LC) are require to turn OFF SCR.
2. It works as AC voltage at the input. 2. It works as DC voltage at the input.
3. It is used in controlled rectifiers, AC voltage controllers etc. 3. It is used in choppers, inverters etc.
4. No power loss takes place during the commutation. 4. Power loss takes place during the commutation.
5. SCR turns OFF due to negative supply voltage. 5. SCR turns OFF due to voltage and current both.
6. Cost of commutation circuit is nil. 6. Cost of commutation circuit is significant.

Methods of Forced Commutation:

Forced commutation circuits can be classified depending upon weather voltage or current is used for commutation.

Sometimes an additional SCR is used for commutating the main SCR. Such techniques are auxiliary commutation method. Based on these classifications the main commutation techniques are,

  1. Self-commutation by resonating load and LC circuit (Load commutation or class A).
  2. Auxiliary voltage commutation (Impulse commutation or class B).
  3. Auxiliary current commutation (Resonant pulse commutation).
  4. Complementary commutation (Class C).
  5. External pulse commutation (Class E).
